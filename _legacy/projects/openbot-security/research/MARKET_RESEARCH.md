# Executive Summary

The U.S. private security services industry exceeds **$70 billion annually** and is growing at 4–6% per year, driven by rising property crime, labor shortages among security guards, and increasing technology adoption. The market for security robots specifically is projected to reach **$3.5–5 billion globally by 2030**, with North America as the dominant region.

OpenBot Security's value proposition — an **affordable, open-source hybrid air+ground robot patrol system** at **$300–500 per ground unit and $300–450 per drone** — represents a **10–50x cost reduction** versus existing enterprise security robot solutions (Knightscope charges ~$7/hour or ~$5,000+/month per robot). This positions OpenBot to attack the massive underserved **SMB and residential market** that cannot afford enterprise security robots or full-time human guards.

Both Tampa, FL and Atlanta, GA present strong initial markets due to:
- **Above-average crime rates** driving security demand
- **Large HOA/gated community populations** with dedicated security budgets
- **Significant commercial real estate** (warehouses, office parks, construction)
- **Business-friendly regulatory environments**
- **Year-round outdoor operational conditions** (with weather hardening considerations)

## 1.6 Local Regulations — Florida

### Florida Recording Laws
- **Two-party consent state for audio:** Florida Statute §934.03 requires all-party consent for intercepting oral communications
- **Video recording:** Generally permitted in public spaces and on private property with owner consent; no expectation of privacy in public areas
- **Key implication for robots:** Ground robots and drones recording **video only** (no audio) in public or on private property with owner's permission = **legal**
- **Audio recording:** Must either have consent of all parties or not capture audio — **recommend video-only default mode** with opt-in audio
- **Voyeurism statute (§810.14):** Prohibits secretly observing into private spaces — robots must not peer into windows/enclosed areas

### Florida Drone Law (§934.50 — Freedom from Unwarranted Surveillance Act)
- **Private use:** A person may NOT use a drone with an imaging device to record images of privately owned real property or its occupants **with intent to conduct surveillance** if the person has a reasonable expectation of privacy, **without written consent**
- **Reasonable expectation of privacy:** Presumed if the person is not observable from ground level at a legal vantage point
- **Key exceptions that benefit commercial security drone patrol:**
  - Aerial mapping in compliance with FAA regulations ✅
  - Delivery of cargo per FAA regulations ✅
  - Images necessary for safe drone operation/navigation ✅
  - **Property owner consent negates privacy restrictions** ✅
- **Implication:** Drone patrols over a client's own property with written consent = **LEGAL**. Cannot surveil neighboring properties. Flight paths must be carefully planned to avoid capturing images of non-consenting neighbors.
- **Penalties:** Civil liability for violations — aggrieved party can seek injunctive relief + damages (actual or $100 per violation + attorney's fees)

### FAA Drone Regulations (Federal)
- **Part 107 certification** required for commercial drone operations
- **Remote ID** required on all commercial drones (effective 2024+)
- **Flight below 400 feet AGL** — standard altitude ceiling
- **Visual line of sight (VLOS)** required unless waiver obtained
- **Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS) waivers:** Available but difficult; key for autonomous patrol
  - FAA has been expanding BVLOS approvals for commercial operations
  - Expect further liberalization by 2027–2028
- **Night operations:** Permitted with anti-collision lighting (visible 3 miles)
- **Operations over people:** Permitted for Category 1–4 drones (under 55 lbs with specific safety features)
- **Airspace restrictions:** Tampa International Airport (TPA) creates restricted zones — must use LAANC for authorization in controlled airspace

### Florida Security Guard Licensing
- If a robot+SaaS service replaces or supplements guard functions, the **company may need a Class B Security Agency License** (FL Dept of Agriculture) — important to verify
- Personnel monitoring SaaS dashboards remotely may need individual security licenses
- **Recommendation:** Consult with FL Dept of Agriculture & Consumer Services early on licensing

- **Year-round outdoor operation:** Feasible with proper hardening (IP67+, thermal management)
- **Operational uptime estimate:** 85–90% (reduced during severe thunderstorms and hurricanes)
- **Seasonal challenges:** June–September is peak rain/lightning/hurricane — also peak construction theft and property crime
- **Competitive advantage:** Human guards are miserable in Tampa heat; robot patrol is indifferent to temperature
- **Post-hurricane market:** Massive demand for property monitoring during evacuation periods — **major differentiator**

## 2.5 Georgia-Specific Regulations

### Georgia Recording Laws
- **One-party consent state for audio:** Georgia Code §16-11-62 — only ONE party needs to consent to recording of oral communications
- **This is MORE permissive than Florida** — audio recording is legal if the robot operator (or the property owner) consents
- **Video recording in public:** Generally unrestricted in areas with no reasonable expectation of privacy
- **Private property:** Recording permitted by property owner/authorized agent without additional consent needed

### Georgia Drone Laws
- **Georgia follows federal FAA Part 107 regulations** — no additional state-level drone restrictions that are more restrictive
- **Georgia Code §16-11-210 to §16-11-215:** Addresses government use of drones (requires warrants for law enforcement surveillance)
- **Private/commercial use:** Governed by FAA; no Georgia state restrictions beyond federal law
- **Local ordinances:** Some cities (Atlanta, Roswell, etc.) may have drone ordinances for parks and public spaces — check local rules
- **Key advantage over Florida:** Georgia does NOT have an equivalent of Florida's §934.50 restricting private drone surveillance — making commercial drone patrol somewhat easier to deploy

### Georgia Security Licensing
- **Private Detective and Security Agencies Act:** Companies providing patrol/security services need licensing through **Georgia Board of Private Detective and Security Agencies**
- **Company license** required if providing "protection of life or property" services
- If the SaaS service includes human monitoring/dispatch, those operators may need individual licenses
- **Recommendation:** Structure as "technology provider" vs. "security company" to potentially avoid licensing — consult Georgia regulatory counsel

## 3.4 Existing Security Robot Companies — Pricing, Failures & Lessons

### Knightscope (NASDAQ: KSCP)

#### Company Profile
- **Founded:** 2013, Mountain View, CA
- **IPO:** January 2022 (NASDAQ: KSCP)
- **Market cap:** ~$51M (Jan 2026) — down dramatically from IPO
- **Revenue:** ~$11.6M/year (TTM as of Q3 FY25) — extremely small for a public company
- **Net loss:** -$29.7M/year (losing ~$3 for every $1 of revenue)
- **Employees:** 71
- **Stock price:** ~$4.45 (Jan 2026) — 52-week range $2.45–$12.70

#### Products
| Robot | Type | Use Case |
|-------|------|----------|
| K1 Hemisphere | Stationary scanner | Entrances, building access |
| K5 | Outdoor patrol (egg-shaped) | Parking lots, campuses |
| K7 | All-terrain (4-wheel) | Larger outdoor areas |
| K3 | Indoor patrol | Offices, hospitals |

#### Pricing Model
- **$7/hour per robot** (~$5,040/month) — positioned just below minimum wage
- **Machine-as-a-Service (MaaS)** model — client leases, doesn't own
- **Includes:** Robot hardware, maintenance, software, KSOC dashboard
- **Contract terms:** Typically 12–36 months

#### Key Failures & Lessons

1. **Financial unsustainability:** Despite 10+ years of operation, Knightscope has NEVER been profitable. Losing $30M+/year on $12M revenue means the MaaS model doesn't work at their cost structure.

2. **High unit cost:** Each K5 costs Knightscope an estimated $50,000–100,000 to build. At $5,040/month revenue, payback is 10–20 months BEFORE operating costs. The hardware is simply too expensive.

3. **PR incidents:**
   - K5 knocked down a toddler in a mall (2016) — major negative press
   - K5 drove into a fountain/pool in Washington DC (2017) — went viral
   - K5 "ignored" a person being attacked (by design — robots are for observation, not intervention) — public backlash
   - These incidents revealed **unrealistic public expectations** about robot capabilities

4. **Slow sales cycle:** Enterprise sales with 6–18 month cycles. Cities and corporations are slow to adopt.

5. **Scaling challenges:** Each robot requires site mapping, customization, and ongoing maintenance. Cannot just "drop and deploy."

6. **Pivot to communications:** Acquired CASE Emergency Systems (blue light towers) in 2022 to diversify — signals core robot business isn't scaling.

7. **Stock price collapse:** From $40+ (split-adjusted) at IPO to $4.45 — retail investors burned.

**Lessons for OpenBot:**
- **MUST achieve low unit cost** ($300–500 is perfect — 100x cheaper than Knightscope)
- **Self-service deployment** is critical — cannot rely on custom site setup
- **Manage expectations:** Position as "deterrence + monitoring" not "robot guard"
- **Recurring revenue** is important but subscription must be affordable ($200–500/month, not $5,000)
- **Open-source community** can provide features, integrations, and maintenance that a small company cannot
- **SMB-first strategy** avoids the enterprise sales cycle death spiral

### Cobalt AI (formerly Cobalt Robotics)

#### Company Profile
- **Founded:** 2016, San Francisco
- **Pivot:** Started as physical robot company; **pivoted to SaaS-only AI platform** (2024-2025)
- **Funding:** $150M+ raised
- **Clients:** DoorDash, Ally Financial, Slack, enterprise buildings

#### Original Robot Product
- Indoor patrol robot with human-in-the-loop monitoring

#### Why They Pivoted Away from Robots
1. **Hardware is hard:** Maintaining a fleet of custom robots across customer sites was operationally brutal
2. **Economics didn't work:** Even at $6–8/hour, couldn't cover hardware + remote operator costs
3. **Customers wanted software:** Clients liked the AI/alerting but were lukewarm on the physical robot
4. **Pivoted to "Cobalt Monitoring Intelligence"** — pure SaaS that connects to existing cameras/sensors
5. **Now calls itself "Cobalt AI"** — removed "Robotics" from the name entirely

**Key Lesson for OpenBot:** The hardware must be **ultra-low-cost and user-maintainable** or else robot economics fail at scale. OpenBot's $300–500 price point and open-source design directly address this.
