## 1. Partnership Structures with Security Company Owner

### 1.1 Co-Founder Equity Split Models

The partnership between a **technical founder** (Dr. Castro — robotics IP, engineering, product development) and an **industry/distribution founder** (neighbor — security company, client relationships, industry expertise, sales channel) maps to well-established co-founder frameworks.

#### Recommended Equity Split Frameworks

| Model | Dr. Castro | Neighbor | Rationale |
|-------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Technical-Heavy** | 60–65% | 35–40% | IP is the core moat; neighbor contributes channel but not full-time effort |
| **Equal Partners** | 50/50 | 50/50 | If neighbor brings equal capital, full-time commitment, and existing client base |
| **Contribution-Weighted** | 55/45 | 45/55 | If neighbor brings significant capital + guaranteed pilot customers |
| **Dynamic (Slicing Pie)** | Varies | Varies | Equity accrues based on tracked contributions over time |

#### Key Factors to Weight

- **Who is full-time first?** The person going full-time earliest typically gets more equity
- **IP contribution:** Dr. Castro's pre-existing robotics IP should be valued as a contribution (often 10–20% premium)
- **Capital contribution:** If neighbor invests cash, that adjusts the split
- **Customer guarantee:** If neighbor commits X existing clients as pilots, that has concrete value
- **Opportunity cost:** Dr. Castro's technical skills are harder to replace than distribution (many security companies exist; few roboticists do)

#### Recommended Starting Point
**60/40 in favor of Dr. Castro** with provisions for the neighbor to earn additional equity through hitting distribution milestones (e.g., +5% upon delivering first 10 paying clients within 12 months).

### 1.2 Licensing / White-Label Model

**Structure:** Dr. Castro builds and owns all technology. Neighbor's security company licenses the robots and software, selling under their existing security brand.

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **IP Ownership** | 100% Dr. Castro / OpenBot entity |
| **License Fee** | Per-unit royalty ($50–100/robot) + % of SaaS revenue (15–25%) |
| **Branding** | Neighbor applies own security company branding |
| **Support** | Dr. Castro handles L2/L3 technical support; neighbor handles customer-facing L1 |
| **Exclusivity** | Possible territorial exclusivity (Tampa metro) for 12–24 months with minimum volume commitments |
| **Pros** | Dr. Castro retains full IP; can license to other security companies nationally |
| **Cons** | Less control over customer experience; neighbor may underinvest in sales |

**Best for:** If Dr. Castro wants to eventually license to many security companies and the neighbor relationship is more arm's-length.

### 1.3 Revenue Share Model

**Structure:** Neighbor provides customers from existing security client base; Dr. Castro provides robots and platform. Revenue is split on each deal.

| Component | Dr. Castro Share | Neighbor Share |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Hardware Sales** | 70% | 30% |
| **SaaS/Monitoring Subscription** | 60% | 40% |
| **Installation/Setup Fees** | 50% | 50% |
| **Maintenance Contracts** | 65% | 35% |

**Pros:**
- Low upfront commitment from both parties
- Performance-aligned incentives
- Easy to unwind if partnership doesn't work

**Cons:**
- Complex accounting
- Potential disputes on cost allocation
- Neither party has full skin in the game

**Best for:** Testing the waters before committing to a deeper structure.

### 1.4 Service Provider / Add-On Model

**Structure:** Neighbor's existing security company adds "Robot Patrol" as a premium service tier alongside human guards, using Dr. Castro's robots.

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Relationship** | Dr. Castro is a vendor/supplier to neighbor's company |
| **Pricing** | Neighbor buys robots at wholesale ($250–400); resells service at markup |
| **Contract** | Supply agreement + service-level agreement (SLA) for maintenance |
| **Customer Relationship** | 100% owned by neighbor's company |
| **Dr. Castro's Revenue** | Hardware margin + monthly per-unit software/support fee ($30–50/robot) |

**Pros:**
- Fastest path to market — leverages existing sales infrastructure
- Neighbor is financially motivated to sell hard
- Dr. Castro can focus purely on product

**Cons:**
- Dr. Castro becomes a supplier, not a brand
- Limited upside if robots become wildly successful
- Dependent on one distributor

**Best for:** If Dr. Castro wants to stay purely technical and the neighbor has a substantial client base (50+ accounts).

### 1.5 Joint Venture vs. LLC Operating Agreement

#### Option A: New LLC (Joint Venture Entity)

Form a **new LLC** (e.g., "OpenBot Security Solutions LLC") separate from both Dr. Castro's robotics work and the neighbor's existing security company.

**Operating Agreement Key Terms:**
- **Members:** Dr. Castro (60%) + Neighbor (40%) — or per agreed split
- **Capital Contributions:** Dr. Castro contributes IP license + sweat equity; Neighbor contributes $X cash + client introductions
- **Management:** Member-managed; Dr. Castro = CTO/Product; Neighbor = CEO/Sales
- **Profit Distribution:** Pro-rata to ownership, quarterly
- **Decision Rights:** Major decisions (>$10K spend, new hires, debt) require unanimous consent; operational decisions by respective domain leads
- **Non-Compete:** Both members agree not to compete in robot security during involvement + 2 years post-departure
- **Buy-Sell (Shotgun Clause):** If one wants out, they set a price; other party can buy or sell at that price
- **Dissolution:** 2/3 vote or automatic trigger if revenue < $X for 12 consecutive months

#### Option B: Dr. Castro's Entity + Service Agreement

Dr. Castro forms his own company (e.g., "OpenBot Robotics LLC"), enters into a **strategic services agreement** with neighbor's existing security company.

| | Joint Venture LLC | Separate Entities + Agreement |
|---|---|---|
| **IP Protection** | IP co-owned or licensed to JV | IP stays 100% with Dr. Castro |
| **Liability** | Shared in one entity | Isolated — each company's liability is separate |
| **Tax Flexibility** | Single K-1 | Each entity files separately |
| **Exit Complexity** | Must dissolve or buy out | Simply terminate agreement |
| **Fundraising** | Easier to pitch one entity | Investors may prefer clean single entity |
| **Recommendation** | If committed long-term | If testing partnership first |

#### ✅ Recommended Approach
**Start with Option B** (separate entities + service agreement for 6–12 months) → convert to **Option A** (Joint Venture LLC) once product-market fit is proven and trust is established. Include a pre-negotiated conversion term sheet in the initial agreement.

### 1.6 Vesting & IP Considerations

#### Vesting Schedule (for Joint Venture)
- **4-year vesting, 1-year cliff** (industry standard)
- Both founders vest equally
- Upon cliff: 25% vested; then monthly vesting for remaining 36 months
- **Acceleration:** Single-trigger on acquisition (partial) or double-trigger on termination without cause after acquisition

#### IP Ownership — Critical Provisions
1. **Pre-existing IP:** Dr. Castro's existing robotics code, designs, and patents remain his property; he licenses them to the JV
2. **Jointly-developed IP:** Any IP created specifically for the JV (custom security features, monitoring software) is owned by the JV
3. **Open-source components:** Clearly delineate which parts are open-source (and under which license — recommend Apache 2.0 or MIT for hardware designs, AGPL for SaaS to protect commercial value)
4. **Assignment on exit:** If a founder leaves, their rights to JV-developed IP remain with the JV
5. **Background IP license:** Dr. Castro grants JV a perpetual, royalty-free license to use his background IP in the security product; license is **non-exclusive** so he can use same tech in other verticals

## 2. Business Models

### 2.1 Hardware Sales (Robot Kits & Assembled Units)

**Description:** Sell ground robots and drones directly to customers as one-time purchases.

| Product | BOM Cost | Sell Price | Margin |
|---------|----------|-----------|--------|
| Ground Robot Kit (DIY) | $180–250 | $399–499 | 50–55% |
| Ground Robot (Assembled) | $250–350 | $599–799 | 45–56% |
| Drone Kit (DIY) | $180–280 | $399–449 | 38–55% |
| Drone (Assembled) | $250–350 | $599–749 | 42–53% |
| Starter Bundle (1 ground + 1 drone) | $400–550 | $899–1,199 | 50–55% |

**Pros:** Simple model, immediate revenue, open-source builds community  
**Cons:** One-time revenue, no recurring income, customer support burden, harder to fund growth

---

### 2.2 RaaS — Robot as a Service

**Description:** Customers pay a monthly fee per robot deployed. OpenBot owns the hardware, handles maintenance and replacements.

| Tier | Robots Included | Monthly Fee | Annual Contract |
|------|----------------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Starter** | 1 ground robot | $199/mo | $2,148/yr (10% discount) |
| **Standard** | 2 ground + 1 drone | $449/mo | $4,848/yr |
| **Premium** | 4 ground + 2 drones | $799/mo | $8,628/yr |
| **Enterprise** | Custom fleet | Custom pricing | Custom |

**Pros:** Predictable recurring revenue, higher LTV, lower barrier to entry for customers, you maintain the fleet  
**Cons:** High upfront capital (you own the robots), maintenance costs, unit economics must work at scale

**Key Metric:** Payback period on hardware cost = 2–4 months at these prices.

---

### 2.3 SaaS — Monitoring Dashboard & Cloud Alerts

**Description:** Sell the software platform as a subscription, independent of hardware. Customers who buy or build their own robots subscribe for the AI monitoring, cloud alerts, mobile app, and analytics.

| Tier | Features | Monthly Price | Annual Price |
|------|----------|--------------|-------------|
| **Basic** | Live video feed, motion alerts, mobile app | $29/mo | $299/yr |
| **Pro** | + AI threat detection, scheduled patrols, video history (30 days), multi-site | $79/mo | $799/yr |
| **Enterprise** | + API access, custom integrations, 90-day history, SLA, dedicated support, SIEM integration | $199/mo | $1,999/yr |
| **White-Label** | Full platform under partner's brand | $499/mo + per-seat fees | Custom |

**Pros:** 85–92% gross margins, scales beautifully, works with DIY/open-source community  
**Cons:** Requires critical mass of users, open-source purists may resist, support overhead

### 2.4 Managed Service (Full Install + Maintain + Monitor)

**Description:** Turnkey solution — OpenBot handles everything: site survey, installation, robot deployment, monitoring, maintenance, and response coordination.

| Package | What's Included | Monthly Fee |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| **Residential** | 2 ground robots, 1 drone, monitoring, maintenance | $349–499/mo |
| **Small Commercial** | 4 ground robots, 2 drones, 24/7 monitoring, maintenance | $799–1,299/mo |
| **Large Commercial** | 8+ ground robots, 4+ drones, dedicated SOC analyst, priority response | $2,499–4,999/mo |

**Pros:** Highest revenue per customer, sticky (hard to switch), premium positioning  
**Cons:** Operationally complex, requires trained technicians, scaling is linear (people-dependent)

### 2.5 Hybrid Model — Hardware Sale + Required SaaS (Ring/Nest Model)

**Description:** Sell robots at accessible prices; require a SaaS subscription for full functionality. Basic offline functionality works without subscription; AI features, cloud storage, and remote monitoring require it.

| Component | Price |
|-----------|-------|
| **Ground Robot Purchase** | $499–699 |
| **Drone Purchase** | $399–599 |
| **Required SaaS Subscription** | $19–79/mo |
| **Optional Premium Features** | +$29–99/mo |

**Example Customer Journey:**
1. Buys Starter Bundle: $999
2. Subscribes to Pro monitoring: $79/mo
3. Year 1 total revenue: $999 + $948 = **$1,947**
4. Year 2+ revenue: **$948/yr** (pure recurring)

**Pros:** Revenue from day one + recurring, customer owns hardware (feels good), lower support burden than RaaS  
**Cons:** Must balance free vs. paid features carefully, piracy/jailbreak risk with open-source hardware

**✅ This is the recommended primary model for the consumer/prosumer market.**

## 4. Go-to-Market Strategy

### 4.1 Leveraging Neighbor's Existing Security Client Base

**This is the #1 unfair advantage.** The neighbor's security company provides:

1. **Warm introductions** to existing clients already paying for security
2. **Trust transfer** — clients trust their current security provider's recommendations
3. **Upsell path** — "We're now offering AI-augmented patrol to complement your guards"
4. **Operational infrastructure** — dispatch, billing, customer service already exists

**Execution Plan:**
1. **Audit neighbor's client list** — Identify top 20 accounts most likely to adopt (HOAs, commercial properties, warehouses)
2. **Tier clients by fit:**
   - Tier 1: Already complaining about guard costs or coverage gaps
   - Tier 2: Have large properties with perimeter security needs
   - Tier 3: Tech-forward decision makers
3. **Personal outreach** — Neighbor introduces Dr. Castro directly to top 5 Tier 1 clients
4. **Pilot offer** — "Try it for 60 days alongside your current guard service at no cost"
5. **Conversion** — After pilot, present data: incidents detected, area covered, cost comparison

---

### 4.2 HOA Board Pitch Strategy

HOA boards are the ideal beachhead customer. They have:
- Annual security budgets ($50K–500K+)
- Democratic decision-making (one board vote = entire community sale)
- Strong word-of-mouth to other HOAs
- Visible deployment = free marketing

**The HOA Board Pitch Deck (Key Slides):**

1. **"Your security spend is broken"** — Show typical HOA guard cost vs. coverage gap
2. **Live demo** — Robot patrols the parking lot during the presentation
3. **Cost comparison** — Current annual spend vs. OpenBot annual cost (90%+ savings)
4. **Per-homeowner cost** — "$5–10/home/month for 24/7 patrol"
5. **Case study** — Pilot results from first deployment (once available)
6. **Risk mitigation** — "Keep your existing guard for gate duty; robots handle the rest"
7. **60-day pilot program** — Zero risk trial
8. **Homeowner satisfaction** — "Your residents will see robots patrolling = visible security"

**Timing:** Present at annual board meetings (typically Q1) when budgets are set. Target budget planning season (October–December) for following year.

**Channel:** Florida Community Association Journal, HOA management companies (FirstService Residential, Associa, Castle Group)

### 4.5 Demo / Pilot Program Design

| Phase | Duration | Activities |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| **Site Survey** | Day 1–3 | Map property, identify patrol zones, WiFi/connectivity assessment |

**Pilot Terms:**
- **Hardware:** OpenBot provides all equipment (robots remain OpenBot property during pilot)
- **Cost to customer:** $0 during pilot; $499/mo Pro tier if they convert
- **Data sharing:** Customer agrees to allow anonymized data use for marketing/case studies
- **Conversion incentive:** Sign annual contract during pilot → first 2 months free
- **Target:** 3–5 pilot sites simultaneously; convert ≥60% to paying customers

**Success Metrics for Pilot:**
- Incidents/anomalies detected per week
- Area covered (miles patrolled)
- Uptime percentage (target: 95%+)
- False alert rate (target: <5%)
- Customer satisfaction survey (target: 8+/10)

### 5.5 FAA Compliance for Commercial Drone Patrol

Based on current FAA Part 107 regulations for small UAS (< 55 lbs):

#### Requirements for Commercial Drone Operations

| Requirement | Details | Status/Action |
|-------------|---------|--------------|
| **Remote Pilot Certificate** | Pass UAG knowledge test, be 16+, English proficient | Dr. Castro + any operators must obtain |
| **Drone Registration** | Register each drone via FAADroneZone; $5/3 years | Required before first flight |
| **Visual Line of Sight (VLOS)** | Pilot must maintain visual contact with drone | **Major challenge for autonomous patrol** — requires waiver |
| **Night Operations** | Allowed under Part 107.29 with anti-collision lighting | Must have visible lighting at 3 statute miles |
| **Operations Over People** | Allowed for Category 1–4 depending on drone specs | Must meet kinetic energy limits or have declaration of compliance |
| **Altitude** | Max 400 feet AGL | Generally fine for patrol (100–200 ft typical) |
| **Speed** | Max 100 mph groundspeed | Not an issue (patrol speed ~15–30 mph) |
| **Airspace** | Controlled airspace requires LAANC authorization | Check each deployment site |
| **Remote ID** | Required for all drones (broadcast identity/location) | Must include Remote ID module |

#### Critical Waiver Needed: Beyond Visual Line of Sight (BVLOS)

**This is the biggest regulatory hurdle for autonomous drone patrol.** Standard Part 107 requires a human pilot to maintain visual contact. Autonomous patrol means BVLOS operations.

**BVLOS Waiver Process:**
1. Apply via FAA DroneZone
2. Demonstrate safety case: detect-and-avoid (DAA) system, ground-based observers, restricted airspace
3. Approval timeline: 90–180 days (improving)
4. Likely conditions: restricted geographic area, max altitude, daylight initially

**Alternative Approaches:**
- **Start with VLOS only** — drone launches, patrols within visual range of base station operator, returns
- **Tethered drone** — physically tethered drones are exempt from some Part 107 requirements (but limited range)
- **Scheduled flyovers** — rather than continuous flight, drone launches on schedule or trigger, flies pre-approved route, returns
- **Partner with existing BVLOS operators** — companies like Skydio, Zipline, and others have obtained BVLOS waivers; may be able to operate under their frameworks

**Timeline:** FAA is actively expanding BVLOS rules. The 2024 NPRM on expanded operations and the FAA Reauthorization Act of 2024 signal more permissive BVLOS rules coming in 2025–2027. Early mover advantage in building compliance infrastructure.
